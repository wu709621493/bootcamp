## 1. Photon-Specific Black Hole Collapse

The collapse of matter (or radiation) into a black hole is described by general relativity. Photons do
contribute to gravitational collapse through their energy density, but there is no known mechanism for a
"photon-specific" collapse that would selectively involve only photons. Any scenario where radiation dominates
would still follow the Einstein field equations, leading to either a stable radiation-dominated star or to
collapse into a black hole if sufficient energy density is confined in a region smaller than its Schwarzschild
radius. The process is governed by the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations or their radiative analogues and is
not instantaneous.

## 2. Collapse Time Scale of $10^{-5}$ Seconds

A collapse time of $10^{-5}$ seconds would imply an extremely small mass and radius if gravitational collapse
were even feasible. The free-fall time $t_{\mathrm{ff}}$ for an object of mean density $\rho$ is given by

$$
 t_{\mathrm{ff}} \approx \sqrt{\frac{3\pi}{32 G \rho}}.
$$

Solving for density gives $\rho \approx 3\pi/(32 G t_{\mathrm{ff}}^2)$. Substituting $t_{\mathrm{ff}} = 10^{-5}$
seconds yields a mean density on the order of $10^{18}\ \mathrm{kg\,m^{-3}}$, comparable to nuclear densities.
While such densities occur in neutron stars, achieving them in a photon gas without exotic matter is not
possible with our current understanding of physics.

## 3. Refractive Index Quantized to Infinity

The refractive index $n$ of a medium is defined as $n = c/v_{\mathrm{phase}}$. An "infinite" refractive index
would imply a phase velocity of zero. No known physical medium forces electromagnetic waves to halt completely
without absorption or conversion to other forms of energy. Moreover, quantization typically refers to discrete
energy levels or modes. The statement "quantized to infinity" is therefore internally inconsistent: an infinite
value cannot be part of a discrete sequence of finite eigenvalues.

In extreme gravitational fields, the coordinate speed of light can approach zero for distant observers, but local
observers always measure light traveling at $c$. Thus, the concept does not align with the physics of curved
spacetime.
